# Introduction

Arbitration is an important method of dispute resolution in China. Instead of bringing a dispute before a court, parties may agree to submit their dispute to an arbitral tribunal whose decision is generally final and binding.

Chinese arbitration law is governed by the Arbitration Law of the People's Republic of China and related judicial interpretations. Arbitration is widely used in commercial disputes because it offers flexibility, confidentiality, and efficiency.

In international business transactions, arbitration is often preferred because arbitral awards can be enforced in many countries. This makes arbitration an attractive option for parties involved in cross-border trade and investment.

This guide provides an introduction to the main principles of Chinese arbitration law, including arbitration agreements, jurisdiction rules, validity of arbitration clauses, and practical case examples.
